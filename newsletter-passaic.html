A Malware Attack Cut All Phone Lines for a New Jersey County Serving 526,000 Residents

On March 5th, Passaic County, NJ lost its entire government phone system to a malware attack — and officials couldn't even tell residents where to call for help. This is what modern cyberattacks look like, and why your business needs to be ready.

Local Impact:  This attack happened in the New York metro area — Passaic County is home to Paterson, Wayne, Clifton and surrounding communities. This threat is not abstract. It is happening in our backyard.

Incident Overview

What Happened in Passaic County

On Wednesday, March 5th, Passaic County, New Jersey confirmed that a malware attack had struck its IT systems, taking down all government phone lines across the county. By Thursday, phones were still down, with no restoration timeline announced and no alternate contact numbers provided to residents.

The county's public alert was striking in what it lacked: no workaround instructions, no alternate numbers, and no details about which systems were affected beyond the phones. Officials acknowledged they were working with state and federal partners to investigate and contain the incident — and noted that several other New Jersey local governments had experienced similar attacks around the same time, though none were identified by name.

As of reporting, the county had not confirmed whether any resident data was accessed, whether systems were encrypted, or whether public safety operations such as 911 or emergency dispatch were affected.

Executive Takeaways

5 Hard Lessons for Business Leaders

  • 1
    Communications infrastructure is a primary attack target. Attackers didn't just lock files — they took out the phones. If your business lost all phone lines today, could you still operate? Could your clients reach you? Could your team coordinate a response?
  • 2
    You need a communication backup plan before an attack — not during one. Passaic County had no alternate numbers to give residents. When your systems go down, you cannot build a backup plan in real time. It must exist before the incident.
  • 3
    Attacks are increasingly coordinated across multiple targets. The county's acknowledgment that several other NJ governments were hit simultaneously points to an organized threat campaign — not random opportunism. Industries and regions can be targeted together.
  • 4
    Transparency gaps create their own crisis. The county's inability to share basic information — alternate contacts, affected systems, data exposure status — eroded public trust. In a business context, clients and partners expect rapid, clear communication when incidents occur.
  • 5
    Federal and state resources don't arrive instantly. Even with government-level support, systems were still down the next day. For businesses, this means you cannot rely on external help arriving in time to prevent operational damage — your first line of defense must be internal.

What This Means for Your Organization

The New Reality: Every System Is a Target

What makes the Passaic County incident particularly instructive is that it wasn't ransomware demanding payment — it was malware designed to disrupt operations. The goal wasn't necessarily financial. It was to take down critical systems and force a crisis response.

For businesses, this expands the threat model significantly. It's no longer just about protecting data from theft. It's about ensuring that your phones, email, client portals, internal tools, and operational systems can withstand or rapidly recover from an attack designed purely to disable them.
