Ransomware Forces a Major Hospital System to Close All 35 Clinics — Chemotherapy Canceled, Doctors Back to Pen and Paper

On February 19th, ransomware hit the University of Mississippi Medical Center — one of the state's largest health systems — taking down its entire network, forcing clinic closures for over 10 days, and putting patients at direct risk. This is what a full-scale cyberattack looks like in the real world.

Incident Date February 19, 2026
Source AP News / CNN / NPR

Brief No. Issue 03 — March 2026
🏥

Patient Safety Impact:  This attack canceled chemotherapy appointments, shut down electronic health records, and forced doctors to treat patients using pen and paper. Ransomware is no longer just a data problem — it is a life-safety crisis.

Incident Overview

What Happened at UMMC

In the early hours of Thursday, February 19th, ransomware struck the University of Mississippi Medical Center — Mississippi's only academic medical center, operating seven hospitals and 35 clinics statewide with over 10,000 employees and a $2 billion annual budget. The attack took down the entire IT network, including the Epic electronic health records system used by doctors, nurses, schedulers, and county health departments across the state.

All 35 clinics were immediately closed. Elective procedures and surgeries were canceled. Chemotherapy appointments were halted. Patients who had driven hours for life-saving treatment were turned away. Doctors reverted to pen and paper to document care. The FBI, Department of Homeland Security, and federal health officials were deployed to assist within hours.

The clinics remained closed for over 10 days before reopening on March 2nd — with extended hours to work through a backlog of canceled appointments. Investigators are still determining whether patient data was compromised.

Event Sequence

How the Attack Unfolded

Thursday, February 19 — Day 1
Ransomware Detected — Full Network Goes Down
The attack hit in the early morning, taking down the Epic electronic health records system and the entire IT network. All clinics closed. Elective procedures and surgeries canceled. FBI and DHS notified. Doctors began treating patients with pen and paper.
Friday, February 20 — Day 2
All Clinics Remain Closed — Chemotherapy Canceled
Closures extended for a second day. Chemotherapy patients, including cancer patients who had driven hours for treatment, were turned away. UMMC confirmed attackers had made contact with the hospital. Federal officials said they had been monitoring the attack since Thursday.
February 21 – March 1 — Days 3–10
Extended Closure — Investigation Continues
Clinics remained closed through the following week. County health departments relying on UMMC's systems were also impacted. Investigators worked to determine whether patient data was accessed. Hospital emergency departments stayed open using manual procedures throughout.
Monday, March 2 — Day 12
Clinics Reopen — Extended Hours to Clear Backlog
After more than 10 days offline, clinics reopened with extended hours. UMMC began contacting patients to reschedule canceled appointments. Data exposure status remained under investigation.

Executive Takeaways

5 Critical Lessons for Every Business Leader

  • 1
    Size and resources are no protection. UMMC has a $2 billion budget, 10,000 employees, and relationships with the FBI and federal government — and still suffered 10+ days of operational shutdown. If it can happen to them, it can happen to any organization.
  • 2
    A cyberattack is an operational crisis, not just an IT problem. This attack didn't just lock files — it canceled cancer treatments, shut down scheduling systems, disrupted billing, and forced clinical staff to work without any digital tools. Every system your business depends on is a potential target.
  • 3
    Recovery takes far longer than most organizations plan for. Experts warn that ransomware recovery now routinely takes weeks to months. UMMC was offline for 10 days — and that was with immediate federal assistance. Does your business have a continuity plan that covers weeks without your core systems?
  • 4
    Downstream organizations are also affected. County health departments across Mississippi that relied on UMMC's electronic records system were disrupted — even though they weren't directly attacked. If your vendors, clients, or partners are hit, your operations can suffer too.
  • 5
    Having a downtime plan in place before an attack saves lives — and businesses. UMMC's emergency protocols — paper charts, manual procedures, dialysis clinic exception — kept critical care running. Businesses that pre-plan for system failure recover faster and lose less. Those that don't are left improvising under pressure.

What This Means for Your Organization

Three Incidents. Three Sectors. One Clear Pattern.

Over the past three months we have covered a ransomware attack on a Pennsylvania school district, a malware attack on a New Jersey county government, and now a ransomware attack on one of the largest hospital systems in the Southeast. Three different sectors. Three different states. The same outcome: operations shut down, communications lost, and organizations scrambling to recover.

The common thread is not the type of organization — it's the reliance on interconnected digital systems with insufficient protections in place. Every business that stores data, processes transactions, serves clients digitally, or relies on networked communications faces the same fundamental exposure.
