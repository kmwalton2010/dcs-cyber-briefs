What Happened

Ten Firms in 48 Hours. The Legal Sector Is in the Crosshairs.

In the opening months of 2026, the INC Ransomware group went on a targeting spree against U.S. law firms unlike anything the legal sector had seen before. Security firm Halcyon tracked the group claiming 10 law firms and legal services organizations on its dark web leak site within a single 48-hour window.

By the time the dust settled, INC Ransom had claimed a total of 20 law firms in 2026 alone. Halcyon's broader tracking puts the total number of ransomware incidents against law firms between 2025 and early 2026 at more than 200.

The 48-hour clustering is what concerns investigators most. When a ransomware group claims 10 firms in two days — all in the same industry — it typically signals a shared upstream compromise of a legal technology provider. It means attackers may have already been inside these firms for weeks or months before the ransomware was deployed.

What is INC Ransomware?

INC Ransomware is a sophisticated ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) operation active since mid-2023. The group exploits known vulnerabilities in Citrix remote access gateways, Fortinet firewalls, and SimpleHelp RMM software — tools widely deployed across law firms and managed IT providers.

Key Facts

Why Law Firms Are the Target

What Makes Law Firms Uniquely Vulnerable

  • Attorney-client privilege creates enormous pressure to pay ransoms quietly
  • Law firms hold sensitive data across multiple clients — one breach exposes everyone
  • Legal tech tools (Citrix, case management, e-discovery) often run on outdated patch cycles
  • Shared managed IT providers create single points of failure across multiple firms
  • Regulatory disclosure obligations create compliance pressure under tight timelines
  • M&A transactions, litigation files, and criminal defense data command premium dark web value
The Wider Impact

The Supply Chain Theory — and What It Means for Your Firm

The 48-hour clustering has led Halcyon researchers to theorize a shared upstream compromise — likely a breach of a legal technology platform used across multiple firms.

Your firm doesn't have to be doing anything wrong to be at risk. You just have to share a technology provider with another firm that was already breached.

A separate FBI advisory confirmed that the Silent Ransom Group is concurrently running a vishing campaign against U.S. law firms. The legal sector is under coordinated assault from multiple ransomware ecosystems simultaneously.

A phishing vulnerability in the Vincent AI legal research platform exposes more than 200,000 law firms to credential theft.

Legal & Regulatory Fallout

What a Ransomware Hit Means for Your Firm's Obligations

Every state bar imposes duties of confidentiality — a breach of client data may constitute an ethical violation requiring prompt client notification and disciplinary reporting.

State breach notification laws may require notifying affected individuals within 30 to 72 hours. Missing that window creates independent liability separate from the breach itself.

INC Ransom's strategy maximizes this pressure — publishing stolen data publicly when victims don't pay forces firms into a race between paying the ransom and managing the disclosure avalanche. The legal liability doesn't end when the ransomware is removed — it begins.

Action Steps

What Your Firm Should Do Right Now

Five Immediate Priorities

  1. Audit every remote access tool immediately. INC Ransom's primary entry vectors are Citrix, Fortinet, and SimpleHelp RMM. Verify latest patched versions and rotate all credentials.
  2. Inventory your legal technology vendors and ask hard questions. Request SOC 2 reports, penetration test results, and incident response procedures from every vendor.
  3. Train every person who answers the phone. The Silent Ransom Group calls staff posing as IT support. Any call requesting remote access or MFA codes needs immediate verification.
  4. Test your backup and recovery capability now. Ransomware groups deliberately destroy backups before encrypting. Offline, air-gapped backups are the only reliable defense.
  5. Document your incident response plan before you need it. Know in advance who to call, which clients require notification, and what your bar association requires.
The Bottom Line

The Legal Sector Is Being Hunted. Not Randomly — Deliberately.

INC Ransom didn't stumble into the legal sector. They chose it. They understand attorney-client privilege and know how to price a ransom that puts firms in an impossible position.

The 48-hour, 10-firm blitz is the story of a criminal enterprise that has mapped the legal sector's shared technology dependencies and is methodically exploiting them.

If your firm uses Citrix, Fortinet, or SimpleHelp — or shares a managed IT provider with other law firms — you are not a hypothetical target. You are a current one.

The question is whether they've already found the door.
